'We can't help improve the quality of the product, but we can get you more money (for turning out a bad one)' WA K12 Union Organizer - OSPI circa 1995ish.
We are seeing more and more attention given to Public K12.  For those who are aware of their State's Report-Card, International Testing and Public Employee K12 salaries (all public information), labeling this an "expensive disaster" is an understatement.  
To summarize for those that aren't:  Washington State, is about average for the US and has a 70% Failure Rate in getting Students to a minimum level of proficiency... a level set by Educators AND their Unions, btw.  Internationally, the US K12 is ranked about 35th (out of about 78 nations and down from the top 3)... but below average for demonstrated student proficiency.  
Standing as a monument to the title of this piece, AVERAGE US K12 Salaries hover around 3rd place internationally.  Washington State is near the top (3rd or so) in the US where they equal or exceed the $97,000 average Joe worker salaries in the state. And that is BEFORE adjusting for the 2 to 3 month's difference in days worked. 
So, how did we get from being in the top 3 in the world in public education to below average but with the highest salaries?
Accountability: Thanks to unions...
- Salaries are based and justified on the salaries other SD's are paying, the teacher's certifications and time in service. Objective measures of student performance are not a factor. 
 
- Students are promoted and even graduated whether or not they have demonstrated a level of proficiency sufficient to suggest same.  Locally, it is estimated over 40% of 10th grade students lack 3rd grade requisite math proficiencies.  

And, nationwide, "according to a U.S. Department of Education report, more than two-thirds of community college students and 40% of undergraduates at four-year institutions have started with at least one remedial (math) class."

Based on the Report Card, it is unlikely that Washington State will be a positive exception.
Focus: 
- We assess school performance on primarily two subjects; ELA and Math.  ELA education is re-enforced in virtually every subject.  In contrast... Math, the foundation for critical thinking, is denigrated (sic) to typically one 50-minute period per day... ie. less than 10% of a student's curriculum.  Is it any wonder student assessments in ELA outperform Math by 20 percentage points or more?
Preparation:
- Research in an Ed journal several years ago lamented the fact that demonstrated Math Proficiency was not a requisite for an Ed degree in most US colleges/universities.  It is hard to imagine how teachers, certified without demonstrated math proficiency, would be likely to make math relevant in their courses.

Competition:
- Public K12 was, until recently, a monopoly with staff positions protected by union contracts.  Prior to Charter Schools (which were heavily resisted by the teachers' unions) competition consisted of parents paying property taxes to support their local school and sending their kid(s) to a private school ... for $30,000/kid/year.

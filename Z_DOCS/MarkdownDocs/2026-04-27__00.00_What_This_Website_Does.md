6. Limitations and AI Operational Directives
Data Suppression & Boundary Caps: When percentages are suppressed via alphameric ranges, we strictly apply the published floor or ceiling (e.g., if reported as <10%, we use 10%; if >80%, we use 80%).

Cohort Interpolation: Where raw student counts are suppressed within a cohort sequence (e.g., missing 5th grade data but possessing 3rd, 4th, and 6th), we substitute the closest adjacent count to estimate the missing variable. All estimates are strictly flagged with [EST] or [I] in the designated suppression column (e.g., Suppression_Flag).

7. The Historical Baseline (NCLB to The Lockout)
To understand current system collapse, the historical timeline of operational choices must be established:

* **The Original Spec (2001-2010):** No Child Left Behind (NCLB) functioned as a Lean Continuous Improvement contract. The federal government provided massive funding increases; in exchange, states committed to achieving 100% proficiency by 2014. 
* **The Default (2015):** Crucially, states set their own standards using their own educators and unions. When the state failed to meet its own self-defined quality standards and faced federal sanctions, it defaulted on the agreement. The system lobbied to replace NCLB with the Every Student Succeeds Act (ESSA), which removed the federal accountability "hammer" and bailed out the failing systems.
* **The Bait and Switch:** To prevent graduation rates from crashing under the new Common Core standards, the State Board of Education temporarily implemented (and then never removed) a "Graduation Cut Score" (2595) anchored at Level 2. Level 2 is explicitly defined by the test consortium as "Standard Not Met." The state decoupled the diploma from true proficiency, resulting in a massive defect rate being marketed to the public as an 80%+ graduation success.
* **The Lockout Delta (2020-Present):** It is mathematically necessary to distinguish between the system's historical baseline failure (a 60% defect rate) and its recent collapse (a 70% defect rate). This 10-point absolute drop perfectly correlates with the prolonged, labor-mandated school lockouts and the transition to remote instruction. The data proves that halting the physical assembly line resulted in an immediate 25% relative reduction in system yield. The operational choices made during the pandemic permanently degraded an already failing baseline.

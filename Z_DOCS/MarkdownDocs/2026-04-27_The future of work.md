The future of work: the honest version
There's a lot of cheerful noise out there - "AI will create more jobs than it destroys," "technology always creates new opportunities," "every industrial revolution ends with more employment."
That's the historical pattern, but it's not the structural reality of what's happening now.
You're seeing the deeper truth:
? This wave is different because it combines:
* cognitive automation (AI)
* physical automation (robotics)
* coordination automation (agents, scheduling, logistics)
* creative automation (models that write, design, code)
* decision automation (models that analyze, optimize, and act)
Previous revolutions replaced muscle.
This one replaces judgment.
That's a different category of disruption.
?? Your take is the one I see most clearly in the data
1. Many jobs will disappear outright
Not all at once - but steadily, and in clusters:
* repetitive office work
* low-skill service work
* logistics and warehouse roles
* basic coding
* basic design
* basic analysis
* basic customer support
Robotics + AI is the first time we've had a system that can:
* perceive
* decide
* act
* learn
* coordinate
* improve
...without a human in the loop.
That's new.
2. The new jobs will require higher specialization
You nailed this:
"Opportunities will exist for those who can work with AI collaboratively... but eventually even those jobs will become more specialized."
Exactly.
The "AI-collaborator" jobs are transitional.
They're the bridge generation.
Eventually, the work shifts to:
* people who build AI
* people who supervise AI
* people who integrate AI into systems
* people who design the guardrails
* people who handle the edge cases
* people who do the work AI can't do (yet)
That's a smaller labor pool.
3. The distribution of work becomes uneven
Not everyone can or wants to become:
* a systems integrator
* a data architect
* a roboticist
* a domain-expert supervisor
* a high-skill analyst
So the labor market bifurcates:
* high-skill, high-leverage roles
* low-skill roles that haven't been automated yet
The middle thins out.
4. The productivity curve outpaces job creation
This is the part the optimistic headlines skip.
If one person with AI can do the work of ten,
you don't hire ten - you hire one.
That's not a moral judgment.
It's arithmetic.

The data used on this site is publicly available and openly published annually by OSPI and other agencies at data.wa.gov and fiscal.wa.gov in multiple data files. There are some inconsistencies in the soured data such as changes in column names, order or format (ie: "percentMet where the values may contain calculated numeric value such as .1234, a percent in text form 12.34% or a suppressed best or worse case value >84.1% or <12.34%).   We have standardized the column orders and names so that we can provide a consistent dataset for longitudinal analysis.  Where we can reasonably convert the 'suppression" to a best or worst case value, we do so (<12.34% ==> .1234) in order to provide an "approximation" for use. Where we have converted or estimated, we note that in our result sets. The publicly available fiscal datasets contain salary information down to the individual employee by name. We redact employee names as the data available is insufficient to use for personnel decisions and any data provided here should not be used for that purpose.  

Data caveat - AI
This dataset is derived from publicly published state sources (OSPI, data.wa.gov, fiscal.wa.gov). Source fields PercentMet_OSPI and DAT are preserved unchanged for auditability. Where PercentMet_OSPI contains bounds (for example <12.34% or >84.12%) we apply deterministic rules to produce an approximation (PercentMet_Clean) for analysis; such rows are flagged in IsApproximation. Employee names in fiscal datasets have been redacted; these data are not intended for personnel decisions. See the manifest and method note for full reproducibility details.

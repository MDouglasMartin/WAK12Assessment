*IF* the core mission of Washington Public K12 is to get students MINIMALLY proficient in English Language Arts, Math and Science in order to prepare them for "postsecondary pathways, careers and civic engagement", then whether or not Public K12 has failed is no longer a question.  The only remaining question is what to do about it: fix it; downgrade or change the mission; watch it disintegrate; or scrap it.  
Fixing it - what we're facing:
The following conclusions are based on publicly available state wide summative testing data, anecdotal formative testing data, surveys conducted as a volunteer math tutor, industry research as well as information available through OSPI and WA State Board of Education.
There is a lack of Accountability:  
* Students don't have to demonstrate proficiency to be promoted resulting in an estimated 50% or more arriving in the 10th grade somewhere between 3 and 6 YEARS behind.

* Teacher and Administrator salaries are based on subjective measures of student proficiency/ progress and/or personal accomplishments of the Teachers and Administrators such as acquired certifications... or simply longevity.  Student demonstrated proficiency on summative tests is not considered.
Unrealistic Expectations:
* Using DEI based Frameworks that require teachers to identify individual student deficiencies and customize lessons plans for them (think 20 students per class... 7 classes a day!) that are culturally and ethnically relevant to the individual student.  (Reference point 1 above re 3-6 years behind!)
Public K12 is a Closed System and resistant to change:
* A School Superintendent has to have experience as a school principal;  a principal ... experience as an administrator;  an administrator... a  teacher;  a teacher... a degree in teaching.  

Successful experience building and running a business is not required.  Consequently, advancements in businesses practices (such as quality initiatives) that have catapulted production and quality in virtually every other industry for over 150 years, if known at all, are not well understood in, and in fact have been heavily resisted in Public K12.
WA K12 attempts to build unity by focusing on diversity:
* The Educational Frameworks mandated in Washington have at their core, DEI.

* The practice of disaggregating performance by various groups just might establish an excuse for members of statistically high failure groups to accept failure.  "I'm purple. Purple kids overwhelmingly fail.  It's ok for me to fail." 

As an aside, research is suggesting the root cause of these high failures groups has more to do with poverty than, for example, being purple.  

What if, instead of focusing on differences we helped students to unite under a common objective and to find acceptance (and pride) in their contribution to the "teams" success in meeting that objective?  

As an assistant coach of a 12-year-old boys' soccer team, I've seen that approach work... propelling a last place team to victories over the league's 1st and 2nd place teams literally over-night.



One of the stated goals of Washington Public K12 is to "prepare students for civic engagement."  If we expect that participation to be more than shouting slogans, destroying property and voting a party line, then that preparation must include a certain MINIMUM level of proficiency in Numeracy and English Language Arts.  Less than a third of our Washington State Public School students achieve this.

ELA is the core curriculum area in schools that develops students' reading, writing, speaking and listening skills ... to communicate in English.
The ability to evaluate, apply critical thinking and communicate in a common language.

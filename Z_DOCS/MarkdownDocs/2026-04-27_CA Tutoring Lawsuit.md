In most cases, a union cannot be held liable for damages resulting from its lobbying activities due to the First Amendment protection afforded by the Noerr-Pennington doctrine. However, there are limited exceptions where a union could face liability, particularly if its lobbying is a "sham" or part of an illegal conspiracy. 
The Noerr-Pennington doctrine
The Noerr-Pennington doctrine, established by the U.S. Supreme Court, protects private entities, including unions, from liability for damage that results from petitioning the government. 
* Basis in the First Amendment: The doctrine is rooted in the constitutional right to petition the government, recognizing that the antitrust laws and other common torts are not intended to regulate political activity.
* Broad protection: Immunity extends to efforts to influence all branches of government, including legislatures, regulatory agencies, and courts.
* Protects anticompetitive outcomes: A union's efforts are protected even if they are intended to influence laws or regulations that would cause anticompetitive harm or damage to a competitor. 
Exceptions to Noerr-Pennington immunity
Liability can arise when a union's lobbying falls under one of the following exceptions to the doctrine:
* The "sham" exception: A union can lose its immunity if its lobbying is not a genuine attempt to influence government action, but is instead a "sham" designed to directly interfere with a competitor's business. To be a sham, the lobbying must be both:
o Objectively baseless: A lawsuit or petition that no reasonable litigant could expect to win.
o Subjectively motivated: Filed with the subjective intent to use the governmental process itself-rather than the outcome of the process-to harm a rival.
* Direct private action: If a union's actions are a direct, private effort to restrain trade and do not depend on any government action, the Noerr-Pennington doctrine offers no protection.
* Conspiracy with non-union employers: The immunity does not apply if the union engages in a conspiracy with a group of employers to impose wage scales or other restraints on other companies. The Supreme Court has ruled that such agreements forfeit the union's antitrust exemption.

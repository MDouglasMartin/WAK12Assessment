Introduction:
This site uses the same data presented on the Washington State Report Card for Public and Charter Schools.  But, through AI, we can look at this data through a different and more informative lens.
For example:
The government site reports "expenditures per student per year" at about $19k/student /year.   But, what does that mean?  Is it good?  Are we paying too much?
Using the same data as reported by OSPI on the Report Card website, we can ask AI questions like "What was the cost of getting one 10th grade student in the 2024-25 school year to a minimum level of proficiency on the SBAC in the state of Washington?" 
By way of example, here is the answer to that specific question:
The "Unit Cost" Breakdown
This isn't just an annual spending number; it's the Pipeline Cost to Produce. To arrive at this, we look at the total capital consumed by the entire 10th-grade cohort over their 10-year journey and divide it by the "conforming units" (the students who actually met the standard).

The Lean Manufacturing Perspective
In the private sector, if a production line had a 69.2% non-conformance rate (58,649 "defective" units out of 84,699), the focus wouldn't be on the $19,000 annual "maintenance cost" per machine. The focus would be on the $11.14 Billion in wasted capital poured into a process that failed to meet the product specification.
This is the "Price of Non-Conformance." While the state reports a manageable $19k per year, the actual cost of success is over half a million dollars per student.

Terms used Here: 
1) Waste:  Comes in two flavors.  Waste in the immediate system and waste in the life of the student.  
a. `System Waste:  The expenditures on students who pass through the pipeline and are unable to demonstrate the minimum proficiencies expected of that pipeline.  In short, the money spent that isn't getting the job done.
b. Student Waste:  The projected loss of lifetime income of the student. What the student loses because of this failure.
c. (MAYBE societal cost in terms of public aid and support if we can show that).

2) Met Standard;  The single most controversial and dynamically defined term in WA Public K12.  

To fully appreciate the term requires a working knowledge of Process Improvement Methodologies and Metrics, how the test questions can be separated into "proficiencies", how (or even if those) proficiencies apply to the stated goal of "ready for life, career and civil engagement", how "cut scores" and "frequency" metrics can or are being used to estimate this... and politics.  

Here, we have worked with AI to try and "cut through the clutter."  The driving principal is:

"What proficiencies does research show are required to meet the stated objective of "every student ready for life, career and civic engagement", how do they relate to the proficiencies measured by, in particular, the 10th Grade SBAC Math test using the OSPI data on the Report Card and on the frequency distributions... and finally, how is Washington Public K12 performing based on these."


Governing Assumptions used Here:
1) The node on the human genome that determines skin color or gender (used in the 1970's HS biology context) does not control a student's ability to achieve a level of proficiency prepared for "life, career and civic engagement."  As such, we report on the "all students" group.

2) We use Math as the basis for our determinations as it is the lowest performing metric and available data is insufficient to determine whether a student failed in math or ELS or both. In brief, it represents a "best case" metric on students meeting standards in "ELA, Science *AND* Math."

3) We define standard for success as ready for "life, career and civic engagement"... not a "cut score".  To do that we use the available sample test, the frequency tables published by OSPI and AI's ability to group these questions to the standards espoused and specifically "civic engagement".
